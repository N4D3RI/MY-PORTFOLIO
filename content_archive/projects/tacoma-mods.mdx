## The goal

Turn a stock 3rd-gen Tacoma into a truck that can disappear for a month. That meant a handful of specific problems: range, storage, shelter, and a way to run a small workshop out of the bed without burning battery.

## What got built

**Bed platform.** A modular aluminum frame welded from 6061 T-slot, sized against CAD models of every plastic tote I already owned. The drawers roll on sealed ball bearings rated for the washboard. Two slots are hot-swappable so I can swap a workshop drawer for a cooler on the way out of town.

**Power.** A 300Ah lithium bank under the rear seat, fed by a 40A DC-DC charger from the alternator and a 200W solar panel on the bed lid. A small inverter delivers 15A at the tailgate for tools. The fuse panel is aircraft-style — one blown fuse shouldn't take down the system, and each circuit gets its own LED.

**Shelter.** A roof-top tent, but with a modified ladder geometry so it folds closer to the cab and doesn't hit the awning when it's up.

## What it taught me

Engineering software and engineering hardware aren't the same thing — but they rhyme. You still fight tolerance. You still test your assumptions by running the system. The difference is that hardware errors are loud and physical, and they force you to be honest about your mental model.

## Lessons I kept

- Build jigs before you build parts.
- Torque specs exist for a reason; the wrench you already own is not a torque wrench.
- A rolling prototype beats a perfect plan. Ship. Drive. Revise.
