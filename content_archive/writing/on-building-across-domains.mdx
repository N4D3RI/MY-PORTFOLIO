The tools change. The logic doesn't.

When I'm writing Python, the feedback loop is fast and forgiving. I change a variable, the test turns green or red, I reload and go again. Errors are quiet and syntactic. The compiler, or the interpreter, or the type checker will draw a polite box around the line that's wrong.

When I'm cutting aluminum, the feedback loop is slower and unforgiving. I measure twice, draw a jig, make the cut, and if I got it wrong I now own a paperweight.

But the underlying activity is the same.

Both are *building a model*, *testing it against reality*, and *revising*. In software the model lives in your head and in the code. In hardware the model lives in your head, in the CAD file, and in the metal. Both become more honest as you iterate on them. Both punish you for caring too much about how pretty the plan was before you started testing it.

This is why I don't draw a hard line between the two. `py-study` and the truck build are separated mostly by material — the rest is a choice of feedback loop.

The things that carry across:

- **Respect the tolerances.** Every system has them. In code it's milliseconds and bytes. In a truck it's torque and thermal margin. In both, you find out where the tolerances are by running past them.
- **Build the jig, not just the part.** A good test harness in code is the same thing as a good drilling template on a workbench. You pay it once, you ship accurately forever.
- **Ship a rolling prototype.** A working ugly version beats a perfect plan every time, because reality will tell you things your plan won't.

The reason I want a personal site for both kinds of work is that I want them shown as equals. One informs the other. Both are the same person thinking.
